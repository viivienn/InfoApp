• Pressure sores or pressure ulcers are injuries to the skin and the underlying tissues, often caused by increased or prolonged contact between the skin and a hard surface (e.g. mattress, wheelchair, clothes).

  • If not treated immediately, this can lead to complications that may progress into deeper structures such as muscles and bones

  • Detecting and providing immediate care during the early development of pressure sores is crucial.
    • Please advise a healthcare professional upon suspicion of pressure ulcers

    Internal risk factors 

    Pressure sores remain a prevalent issue in long-term care settings.

  • Dehydration and poor nutrition: often lead to brittle and thin skin
    • May be due to dysphagia/difficulty swallowing, lack of appetite, resistance to feeding
    • Reminding all residents to be hydrated is the first step to preventing pressure wounds!
  • Older age: susceptible to skin fragility and slow wound healing
  • Reduced mobility
  • Incontinence
  • Chronic diseases (such as diabetes) 

    • External risk factors


  • Prolonged contact against skin from being seated or lay down for an extended period 
  • Rubbing motion from one surface to another from transfers, position changes and sitting in a wheelchair
    • Use patting motion instead of rubbing when drying the resident’s skin

  • Sweating and incontinence
    • Keep the sheets and the resident’s skin clean and dry
