• Remain patient and provide reassurance when communicating with a resident.
    • Repeat yourself if the resident does not understand what you are trying to say

  • Avoid any kind of confrontation or disagreement with a resident. Avoid interrupting the resident, especially to correct something they said. 
    • Instead, validate their emotions and what they are saying

  • Pay attention to any verbal and non-verbal signs that can give you an idea of what the resident wants. This can help prevent potential distress behavior from the resident.

  • Many residents can become confused as they have been spending their time in their room for many weeks. There are certain questions you can ask to help orient them:
    • What day is it today?
    • What is the weather like outside?
    • How did you sleep last night?
    • What did you eat today?

  • DO NOT speak in a high pitched tone resembling a voice you would use with a young child 

  • DO NOT use inappropriate terms of endearment, such as honey or sweetheart

    To establish contact with the resident:

  • Introduce yourself when you are entering the room

  • Residents are addressed as they wish: e.g. first name, Mr., Ms. or Mrs

  • Establish adequate eye contact to capture the resident’s attention 
    •  Ensure the resident can see, hear, and understand what you are saying

  • Explain the reason for your visit clearly: using simple words and speaking calmly
    • Obtain the resident’s consent prior to your interventions 
    • You may ask if they’re comfortable, if everything is alright, if they need anything
